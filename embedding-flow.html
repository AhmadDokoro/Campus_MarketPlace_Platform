Embedding Feature — How It Works

Campus Marketplace Platform · AI-Powered Product Recommendations

1
Seller
Seller fills in the Add Product form
The seller enters the product title, category, description, price, and image, then clicks Submit.
Backend receives the form
▼
2
Backend (Spring Boot)
Builds a description text for the product
The backend combines the product's title, category name, and description into one single piece of text.

Example: "iPhone 13 Electronics Great condition, barely used phone"
Sends text to OpenAI Embedding API
▼
3
OpenAI Embedding API
Converts the text into a list of numbers (a vector)
OpenAI reads the text and returns 1,536 decimal numbers that together represent the "meaning" of that product — this is called an embedding vector.

Example: [0.023, -0.041, 0.117, 0.009, -0.082, ...]

Think of it like GPS coordinates, but in 1,536 dimensions.
Every product gets its own unique coordinate. Products that are similar in meaning (e.g. two phones) end up at coordinates that are very close together. Products that are different (e.g. a phone vs a textbook) end up far apart.

Backend stores the numbers
▼
4
Database (MySQL)
The vector is saved alongside the product
The 1,536 numbers are serialized to JSON and stored in the embedding column of the products table — right next to the product's title, price, and image.

If the API call fails for any reason, the product is still saved normally — just without an embedding.
1
Buyer
Buyer clicks on a product to view it
The buyer opens a product detail page. At the bottom of that page, the system needs to show similar products.
Backend loads the product's stored embedding
▼
2
Backend
Retrieves this product's embedding from the database
The backend reads the embedding column for the product the buyer just opened — getting back its 1,536 numbers.
Fetches all other products' embeddings
▼
3
Database
Returns the embeddings of every other product
All other products that have an embedding stored are returned — just their ID and their 1,536 numbers. No heavy data yet.
Compares using Cosine Similarity
▼
4
Backend — Cosine Similarity
Calculates how similar each product is to the current one
For every other product, the backend runs a cosine similarity calculation — comparing its vector against the current product's vector. The result is a score from 0.0 (completely different) to 1.0 (identical meaning).

Cosine similarity = measuring the angle between two GPS coordinates.

If two products have coordinates that point in nearly the same direction → small angle → score close to 1.0 → very similar.

If two products point in completely different directions → large angle → score close to 0.0 → not similar.

Example: "iPhone 13 Electronics" vs "Samsung Galaxy Phone" → score ≈ 0.87 (very similar)
Example: "iPhone 13 Electronics" vs "Calculus Textbook" → score ≈ 0.21 (not similar)

Sorts scores high → low, picks top 5
▼
5
Backend
Sorts all scores and picks the top 5
All similarity scores are sorted from highest to lowest. The top 5 product IDs are taken — those are the most semantically similar products to the one the buyer is viewing.
Fetches full details for those 5 products
▼
6
Buyer sees the result
Similar products are shown at the bottom of the page
The backend fetches full product data (title, price, image) for the top 5 IDs, preserves the similarity order, and renders them as the "You may also like" section on the product detail page.
